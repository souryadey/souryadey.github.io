Biography

Sourya Dey obtained his Bachelor of Technology (Honours) degree in Instrumentation Engineering from the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Kharagpur in 2014, along with the Best Undergraduate Thesis award in his department. He joined the University of Southern California (USC) to pursue PhD studies and was a recipient of the Viterbi Graduate School PhD Fellowship. Sourya is currently a PhD candidate scheduled to earn his doctorate in May 2020. He plans to stay in academia beyond that and is currently seeking post-doctoral and faculty positions.

Research

Souryaâ€™s research can be summarized by the title of his upcoming thesis — Exploring Complexity Reduction in Deep Learning. He developed and analyzed a technique known as pre-defined sparsity, which can significantly reduce the complexity of certain neural networks with minimal performance degradation. He received an award for a side project on developing a family of synthetic datasets of tunable difficulty for machine learning systems. Sourya's current project is on developing a search framework to automate the design of a family of networks which trade off performance and training time. Check out this video for a 15-minute glance summary of his research.

Sourya was involved in the inception of a machine learning group at USC in 2016, and subsequently helping it grow via publishing several first-author papers, one of which won a Best Paper award, assisting in writing multiple successful funding proposals, and presenting posters at various events. For complete details and links, see the Research section.

Sourya was previously involved in the field of circuits and systems. He worked on both analog and digital design during his undergraduate program, and his initial PhD research until 2015 was on a non-uniform sampling analog-to-digital converter. Some of his subsequent machine learning work involved an optimized hardware architecture for training sparse neural networks. However, Sourya has now completely switched over from hardware to software, and his current and future research interests include applying automated deep learning, interpretability of machine learning systems, and data science.

For what it's worth, here's my take on Is pursuing a PhD really worth it? — a question which seems to get asked about (or at least thought about) a lot:
Like so many other big questions in life, the correct answer would be It depends. If I were to threshold the answer and make it a Yes/No, I would say Yes. A PhD is like getting a regular industry job, except that it pays less and gives you more freedom. And it lets you be a student, which, to me, feels like a nice slap in the face of I'm a grownup professional now and should act like it.
